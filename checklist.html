Intervention Mapping checklist
http://interventionchecklist.com
Text by Sylvia Roozen & Gjalt-Jorn Peters, based on work by Rik Crutzen, Gerjo Kok, Gjalt-Jorn Peters & Louk Peters, coded by Gjalt-Jorn Peters. This checklist is a product of the Academy of Behavior Change (A-BC).

Introduction

Intervention Mapping (IM) is a protocol that is used for planning theory- and evidence-based health promotion programs based on the best current theory and evidence. The purpose of IM is to provide those planning health promotion programs with a framework for effective decision making at each step in the process of intervention development, implementation, and evaluation. IM is a planning approach that is based on using both theory and evidence as foundations for decision making. It takes an ecological approach to assessing and intervening in health problems and engendering community participation. IM was developed in reaction to a lack of available comprehensive frameworks for health promotion program development. It was designed to guide health promoters in developing the best possible intervention. More specifically, IM ensures that theoretical models and empirical evidence guide planners in two areas: (1) the identification of determinants related to behavioral and environmental causes of a target problem, and (2) the selection of appropriate theoretical methods and practical applications that can be used to address these identified determinants.

IM provides a vocabulary for needs assessment, program planning, procedures related to planning activities, and technical assistance by identifying theory-based determinants and matching them with appropriate methods for change. The IM protocol describes the iterative path from problem identification to problem-solving or mitigation. Each of the six steps of IM comprises several tasks, each of which integrates theory and evidence. The completion of the tasks within a step creates a product that is the guide for the subsequent step. The completion of all of the steps serves as a blueprint for the design, implementation, and evaluation of an intervention based on a foundation of theoretical, empirical, and practical information. The six steps (and related tasks) of the IM process are as follows. Step 1 is the development of a logic model of the problem, and involves conducting a needs assessment or problem analysis by identifying what, if anything, needs to be changed, and for whom. Step 2 involves setting program outcomes and objectives for each stage of the logic model of change. This entails creating matrices of change objectives by combining (sub-)behaviors with behavioral determinants in order to identify which beliefs should be targeted by the intervention. Step 3 is program design, that is, selecting theory-based intervention methods that match the determinants into which the identified beliefs aggregate, and translating these into practical applications that satisfy the parameters for effectiveness of the selected methods. Step 4 focuses on program production, that is, integrating the practical applications into an organized program. Step 5 involves developing a program implementation plan for the adoption, implementation, and sustainability of the program in real-life contexts. This entails identifying program users and supporters and determining what their needs are, and how these needs can be met. Step 6 involves the production of an evaluation plan so that effect and process evaluations can be carried out to measure program effectiveness. IM is a helpful tool that can be used to design health promoting programs in a systematic and evidence-based manner in order to increase the chance of success in reducing prenatal alcohol exposure and FASD. It is true that it is a complex and time consuming process, reflecting the difficulty of changing health behaviors. IM has helped to bring the development of interventions to a higher level, indicating that the advantages associated with this approach outweigh any disadvantages.

Aim

This checklist encompasses all six steps as described in the IM framework. This checklist can be used as a check whether your program or intervention included the most important components. Moreover, the checklist provides you with directions to learn more about unclear concepts or steps in the process.

Note; the checklist is not a program development tool. The checklist serves merely as a quick tool to check upon your current intervention or program. Therefore, the checklist should not be used as a tool to assist you in program developed. Also, the checklist does not provide program developers with suggestions to improve upon an already existing intervention or program and should therefore not be used as an evaluation tool.

How to use this checklist

One of the team members of the planning group should be knowledgeable about behavior change techniques, planning health promoting programs or ideally Intervention Mapping. The checklist consists of six parts (IM step 1 to 6) which follow subsequently. For each part, categories appear to indicate the type of questions that will follow. For each question, the program developer can indicate if the checklist question was included ("Did this"), not included ("Did not do this"), or not applicable for the program ("Not applicable").

In case anything is unclear, directions are provided to find more information and examples in the IM book (incl. page numbers). Moreover, definitions of the most important concepts are provided in the glossary on top of the checklist.

Participatory Planning Group

Did you establish a participatory planning group (PPG)?
A participatory planning group allows participation by community members, potential program implementers, and program beneficiaries in the planning proces. Other forms of information gathering from members external to the intervention developers and researchers can also be considered as fulfilling a similar role.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 214 and further.

Expertise and represention in the PPG

Did you ensure the PPG contains expertise in the health problem or its causes?
Intervention developers are often not also experts regarding the health problem. Therefore, it is important that at least one such expert is included in the PPG. These will often, but not always, be medical experts. In some cases, of course, it is possible this is the same person as the intervention developer.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 216 and further.
Did you secure at least one target population member (or target population experts/proxy) in PPG?
Members of the target population have a unique perspective, and including them in the PPG can often preempt some mistakes. It therefore pays off to discuss program ideas with them regularly.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 215 and further.
Did you ensure the PPG contains diverse perspectives from the target population?
When composing a PPG, a common pitfall is to recruit volunteers from the target population as PPG members. However, such volunteers tend to be positively biased towards the initiative from the outset, and therefore may not be in the best position to provide a critical perspective. For example, they may overestimate how easily peers will adopt the intervention, or they may not be familiar with all practical obstacles people encounter. Therefore, make sure that your PPG also contains target population members who are not unequivocally positive.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 216 and further.
Did you secure at least one prospective implementer in PPG (if applicable)?
Like members from the target population, implementers have a unique perspective. They can foresee pitfalls that may hinder effective implementation in a very early stage. Therefore, it is useful to include them in the PPG as well.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 217 and further.
Did you secure at least one stakeholder in PPG (if applicable)?
In addition to target population members and implementers, other relevant stakeholders may exist. A stakeholder is defined as any individual or organisation that has one or more of power, legitimacy and urgency. Power is defined as formal authority or control. Legitimacy means that the stakeholder is acting in compliance with social norms and expectations. Urgency implies that the stakeholder calls for immediate attention. It is important to identify these and include them in the PPG, as well.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 217 and further.
Did you ensure the PPG contains diverse perspectives from the implementers?
When composing a PPG, a common pitfall is to recruit volunteers from the pool of potential implementers as PPG members. However, such volunteers tend to be positively biased towards the initiative from the outset, and therefore may not be in the best position to provide a critical perspective. For example, they may overestimate how easily peers will implement the intervention, or they may not be familiar with all practical obstacles people encounter. Therefore, make sure that your PPG also contains prospective implementers who are not unequivocally positive.

Populations

Did you define at-risk population?
In your description of the at-risk population (the population that will benefit from this intervention), it is important to clearly describe the population in terms of a combination of characteristics, e.g. age, gender, etc.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 229 and further.
Did you define target population (will often be the same as the at-risk population)?
Similarly, in your description of the target population (the focal population of this intervention, in other words, the population that will be targetted by this intervention), it is also important to clearly describe the population in terms of a combination of characteristics, e.g. age, gender, etc.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 229 and further.

Logic model of the problem

Logic model of the problem: variables

Quality of Life of at-risk population
Did you define Quality of Life of at-risk population?
In your description of the health problem, it is important to clearly define the most relevant variable(s) compromising the Quality of Life for this at-risk population and society.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 232 and further.
Did you assess the quality of life of at-risk population?
In your description of the Quality of Life of the at-risk population, it is important to describe the level of Quality of Life in the at-risk population for each variable that indicates quality of life.
(Health) problem(s) of at-risk population
Did you define (health) problem(s) of at-risk population?
In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to clearly describe what the health risk or problem is. Basic questions such as 'what is the problem?', 'who has it?' assist you in defining the health problem.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 232 and further.
Did you assess prevalence of problem(s) in at-risk population?
In your description of the prevalence, frequency and/or intensity of the at-risk population, it is important to describe the assessment of the prevalence for each (health) problem.
Undesirable behavior(s) of target population
Did you define undesirable behavior(s) of target population?
In your description of behavior(s) of the target population(s) it is important to describe the undesirable behavior(s) in light of the health problem(s) of the at-risk population.Undesirable behavior(s) are those behavior(s) you want to change with your intervention.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 236 and further.
Did you assess the extent of undesirable behavior in target population?
In your description of the undesirable behavior(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to assess the extent in which these behavior(s) are present in target population (e.g., by measuring the prevalence, frequency, intensity).
Environmental factors of at-risk population
Environmental factors contributing to problem(s)
Did you define environmental factors contributing to problem(s)?
In your description of the health problem(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to define environmental factors contributing to these problem(s).
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 237 and further.
Did you identify environmental agent(s) of environmental factor(s) of problem?
In your description of the health problem(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to identify the environmental agent(s) who control the previously described environmental factor(s) of the problem.
Did you assess environmental factors contributing to problem(s)?
In your description of the environmental factors of the at-risk population, it is important to describe the assessment of each environment factor contributing to the (health)problem(s) of the target population.
Environmental factors contributing to QOL
Did you define environmental factors contributing to QOL?
In your description of environmental factor(s) contributing to the undesirable behavior(s), it is important to define to what extend these are contributing to the quality of life of the at-risk population.
Did you identify environmental agent(s) of environmental factor(s) of QOL?
In your description of the health problem(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to identify the environmental agent(s) who control the previously described environmental factor(s) of Quality of Life of the at-risk population.
Did you assess environmental factors contributing to QOL?
In your description of the environmental factors of the at-risk population, it is important to describe the assessment of each environment factor contributing to the Quality of Life in the at-risk population.
Environmental factors contributing to undesirable behavior
Did you define environmental factors contributing to undesirable behavior?
In your description of the undesirable behavior(s) contributing to health problem(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to define environmental factors contributing to these undesirable behavior(s).
Did you identify environmental agent(s) of environmental factor(s) of undesirable behavior?
In your description of the health problem(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to identify the environmental agent(s) who control the previously described environmental factor(s) of the undesirable behavior(s) of the at-risk population.
Did you assess environmental factors contributing to undesirable behavior(s)?
In your description of the environmental factors of the at-risk population, it is important to describe the assessment of each environment factor contributing to undesirable behavior(s) of the target population.

Logic model of the problem: links

Did you justify (health) problem(s)-Quality of Life link(s)?
In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the (health) problem(s) of the at-risk population and the Quality of Life of the at-risk population.
Did you justify behavior(s)-problem(s) link(s)?
In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the (health) problem(s) of the at-risk population and the undesirable behavior(s) of the target population.
Did you justify behavior(s)-QOL link(s)?
In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the undesirable behavior(s) of the target population and the Quality of Life of the at-risk population.
Did you justify environmental factor(s)-undesirable behavior(s) link(s)?
In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the undesirable behavior(s) of the target population and the environmental factor(s).
Did you justify environmental factor(s)-QOL link(s)?
In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the environmental factor(s) and the Quality of Life of the at-risk population.
Did you justify environmental factor(s)-health problem(s) link(s)?
In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the environmental factor(s) and the (health) problem(s) of the at-risk population.
Did you generate a logic model of the problem?
A logic model of the problem presents a conceptual map containing all relevant factors related to the (health) problem(s) you identified.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 259 and further.

Community Capacity

Community Capacity Capacity

Did you identify the target community capacity?
In your description of the target community capacity, it is important to identify a list of the assets contributing to the capacity of the target population.
Did you assess capacity of target community?
In your description of the target community capacity, it is important to describe the assessment of the extent of the capacity of the target population.

Community Capacity Environmental Assets

Did you identify target community environmental assets?
In your description of the target community capacity, it is important to identify a list of the assets contributing to environmental assests of the target population.
Did you assess available environmental assets of target community?
In your description of the available environmental assets of the target community, it is important to describe the ubiquity of each available environmental asset.

Program Goals

Did you define program goals?
In your description of program goals, it is important to define goals in terms of Quality of Life, (health) problem, behavior, relevant environmental factor, and/or determinant of behavior.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 254 and further.
Did you justify program goals?
In your description of program goals, it is important to justify the goals by describing any explanation of the reasoning underlying each program goal.
Did you define specific program goals?
In your description of program goals, it is important to define the program goals as specific as possible.
More information in the Intervention Mapping book at page 256 and further.
Did you define measurable program goals?
In your description of program goals, it is important to define how the program goals are measurable (this also related to the evaluation).
Did you define temporally specific program goals?
Similarly, in your description of program goals it is also important to define temporally specific progam goals. In other words, it needs to be clear in which period/term the goals are to be achieved.

Did you make a decision how to evaluate the program goals?

In your description of program goals, it it important to describe the methodology, operationalisations, from whom data will be gathered, and execution of evaluating the goals. Moreover, it is important to justify if this aspect of the intervention will not be evaluated.

Have you selected an operationalisation for this evaluation, survey, or experiment?
An operationalisation is what is used to measure the variable of interest. This is often a questionnaire, but other measures such as blood values can also be used.
Have you determined from whom you will gather data?
In evaluations, the data are often provided by the target populations, for example, when measuring determinants. However, sometimes it can be useful if another group provides the data; for example, parents or teachers can provide data about their children, or physicians about their patients.
Have you selected the methodology to use for this evaluation, survey, or experiment?
Most research questions can be answered with a number of different methodologies. For example, for effectiveness studies, one will often use experiments (i.e. RCTs), but sometimes, qualitative studies or surveys can be better.
Did you execute this evaluation, survey, or experiment?
Execution of a study means that the data are collected, analyses, and interpreted.
If this aspect of the intervention is not evaluated, did you provide a justification?
Sometimes, evaluation is not possible or desirable. In that case it is important to document why this decision was taken.

Glossary (click here to toggle definitions)
Agent Individuals or groups in positions to exercise control over (aspects of) the environment.
Assess Determine the value of one or more variables
At-risk population The population that will benefit from this intervention
Component A component is a product of the intervention development. The components together form the intervention.
Define Specify what (which variables) was (or were) used/chosen/selected
Delivery channels and vehicles The vehicle is the medium carrying the intervention, for example text, audio, video, and/or interpersonal communication. Old archaic definition of "channel" was that it was either "interpersonal" (of the intervention is delivered to the target population members by an individual) or "mediated" (all other methods to deliver the intervention).
Determinant A modifiable psychological variable
Element NA
Environmental factor Aspects of the environment which influences the behavior
Logic Model A visual representation of the relevant variables and the hypothesized causal relationships between those variables
Participatory Planning Group (PPG) A participatory planning group allows participation by community members, potential program implementers, and program beneficiaries in the planning proces.
Performance objective A sub-behavior of the target behavior. Performance objectives are distinguished on the basis of their similarity in terms of the determinants and enviromental factors that predict them: only if two sub-behaviors are predicted by different determinants and/or environmental factors, they are considered separate performance objectives.
Program goal NA
Quality of Life (QOL) The general state of well-being (health, comfort, and happiness) experienced by an individual or group
Scope A program scope defines breadth of a program. The scope is a list of domains that are included (targeted) by the program as well as a list of domains that are explicitly not included (targeted) by the program. Note that one of these lists can be redundant.
Sequence For programs that consist of more than one component, the sequence defines in which order these must be presented.
Stakeholder Every person, organisation or role whose involvement in the intervention development process can reasonably be expected to contribute to intervention effectiveness.
Sub-determinant An aspect of a determinant that is sufficiently specific. It is important to note that these determinants have a given level of psychological aggregation. For example, in the RAA, the determinant intention is predicted by the three lower-level variables attitude, perceived norm and perceived behavioural control. Attitude, in turn, can be considered to consist of experiential and instrumental attitude; and each of these consists of behavioural beliefs that differ for each target behaviour and each target group. In other words, determinants of behaviour can be organised on the aggregation level hierarchy in terms of specificity versus generality, and various sub-determinants can often be distinguished for any determinant. Sub-determinants formulated at a high level of specificity (e.g., beliefs in the case of RAA) are crucial when studying determinants, because those very specific aspects are what is used in operationalisations and intervention messages.
Target population The population that will benefit from this intervention
Theme A program theme is a general organising construct or idea for a program that manifests as recurring visual and linguistic patterns. It often includes a program's name and potentially a slogan, the "brand" or "corporate identity" (fonts, colors, logo's), and should be tailored to (sub-)cultural characteristics of the target population.
Variable A measurable characteristic of for example a person, situation, object
