Introduction

This checklist contains tasks to undertake when developing an intervention program. It was based on a thorough analysis of the Intervention Mapping (IM) protocol, a comprehensive protocol for the development and analysis of theory- and evidence-based health promotion programs. There are two ways to use this checklist.

  • For professionals trained in Intervention Mapping, as a memory aid to make sure nothing is overlooked. Intervention Mapping is so powerful because it is so comprehensive, and of course an inevitable downside of this comprehensiveness is that it becomes easy to forget something. This checklist can be used to try and avoid that.
  • For professionals who are as yet unfamiliar with Intervention Mapping, but who do have experience with developing health promotion programs, the checklist can serve as an introduction to the Intervention Mapping vocabulary and protocol. Experts in health promotion will often apply the same principles outlined in the Intervention Mapping protocol without using the same terminology, because Intervention Mapping is for a large part a compilation of best practices. It is nonetheless useful to become familiar with Intervention Mapping. First, since Intervention Mapping is increasingly seen as the golden standard in health promotion and behavior change, it is useful to master it's vocabulary to describe one's activities so as to enable convenient communication with other professionals. Second, this comprehensive list can help an intervention developer to avoid overlooking aspects of the intervention development process.

Intervention Mapping

Intervention Mapping is a protocol that is used for planning theory- and evidence-based health promotion programs based on the best current theory and evidence. The purpose of Intervention Mapping is to provide those planning health promotion programs with a framework for effective decision making at each step in the process of intervention development, implementation, and evaluation. Intervention Mapping is a planning approach that is based on using both theory and evidence as foundations for decision making. It takes an ecological approach to assessing and intervening in health problems and engendering community participation. Intervention Mapping was developed in reaction to a lack of available comprehensive frameworks for health promotion program development. It was designed to guide health promoters in developing the best possible intervention. More specifically, Intervention Mapping ensures that theoretical models and empirical evidence guide planners in two areas: (1) the identification of determinants related to behavioral and environmental causes of a target problem, and (2) the selection of appropriate theoretical methods and practical applications that can be used to address these identified determinants.

IM provides a vocabulary for needs assessment, program planning, procedures related to planning activities, and technical assistance by identifying theory-based determinants and matching them with appropriate methods for change. The Intervention Mapping protocol describes the iterative path from problem identification to problem-solving or mitigation. Each of the six steps of Intervention Mapping comprises several tasks, each of which integrates theory and evidence. The completion of the tasks within a step creates a product that is the guide for the subsequent step. The completion of all of the steps serves as a blueprint for the design, implementation, and evaluation of an intervention based on a foundation of theoretical, empirical, and practical information. The six steps (and related tasks) of the IM process are as follows.

  • Step 1 is the development of a logic model of the problem, and involves conducting a needs assessment or problem analysis by identifying what, if anything, needs to be changed, and for whom.
  • Step 2 involves setting program outcomes and objectives for each stage of the logic model of change. This entails creating matrices of change objectives by combining (sub-)behaviors with behavioral determinants in order to identify which beliefs should be targeted by the intervention.
  • Step 3 is program design, that is, selecting theory-based intervention methods that match the determinants into which the identified beliefs aggregate, and translating these into practical applications that satisfy the parameters for effectiveness of the selected methods.
  • Step 4 focuses on program production, that is, integrating the practical applications into an organized program.
  • Step 5 involves developing a program implementation plan for the adoption, implementation, and sustainability of the program in real-life contexts. This entails identifying program users and supporters and determining what their needs are, and how these needs can be met.
  • Step 6 involves the production of an evaluation plan so that effect and process evaluations can be carried out to measure program effectiveness.

Aim of this checklist

This checklist encompasses all six steps as described in the IM framework. This checklist can be used as a check whether your program or intervention includes the most important components. Moreover, the checklist provides you with directions to learn more about unclear concepts or steps in the process.

Note; the checklist is not a program development tool. The checklist serves merely as a quick tool to check your current intervention or program. Therefore, the checklist should not be used as the primary tool for program development. Also, the checklist does not provide program developers with suggestions to improve upon an already existing intervention or program and should therefore not be used as an evaluation tool. The Academy of Behavior Change is involved in the development of Creatisy, an suite of online applications to support systematic development of behavior change programs. However, this product is as yet still under development.

How to use this checklist

One of the team members of the planning group should be knowledgeable about methods for behavior change (of behavior change techniques, BCTs), planning health promoting programs and ideally, Intervention Mapping. The checklist consists of six major sections (Intervention Mapping step 1 to 6) which follow subsequently. Each section contains a number of subsections that organise the checklist elements. For each element, the program developer can indicate whether the corresponding task was performed ("Did this"), omitted ("Did not do this"), or not applicable for the program ("Not applicable").

In case anything is unclear, directions are provided to find more information and examples in the Intervention Mapping book (incl. page numbers for the fourth edition). Moreover, definitions of the most important concepts are provided as 'tooltips' that open up when the mouse cursos hovers over a term, as well as in the glossary which can be opened using the link in the black bar on top of the checklist.

Did you establish a participatory planning group (PPG)?A participatory planning group allows participation by community members, potential program implementers, and program beneficiaries in the planning proces. Other forms of information gathering from members external to the intervention developers and researchers can also be considered as fulfilling a similar role.
Did you ensure the PPG contains expertise in the health problem or its causes?Intervention developers are often not also experts regarding the health problem. Therefore, it is important that at least one such expert is included in the PPG. These will often, but not always, be medical experts. In some cases, of course, it is possible this is the same person as the intervention developer.
Did you secure at least one target population member (or target population experts/proxy) in PPG?Members of the target population have a unique perspective, and including them in the PPG can often preempt some mistakes. It therefore pays off to discuss program ideas with them regularly.
Did you ensure the PPG contains diverse perspectives from the target population?When composing a PPG, a common pitfall is to recruit volunteers from the target population as PPG members. However, such volunteers tend to be positively biased towards the initiative from the outset, and therefore may not be in the best position to provide a critical perspective. For example, they may overestimate how easily peers will adopt the intervention, or they may not be familiar with all practical obstacles people encounter. Therefore, make sure that your PPG also contains target population members who are not unequivocally positive.
Did you secure at least one prospective implementer in PPG (if applicable)?Like members from the target population, implementers have a unique perspective. They can foresee pitfalls that may hinder effective implementation in a very early stage. Therefore, it is useful to include them in the PPG as well.
Did you secure at least one stakeholder in PPG (if applicable)?In addition to target population members and implementers, other relevant stakeholders may exist. A stakeholder is defined as any individual or organisation that has one or more of power, legitimacy and urgency. Power is defined as formal authority or control. Legitimacy means that the stakeholder is acting in compliance with social norms and expectations. Urgency implies that the stakeholder calls for immediate attention. It is important to identify these and include them in the PPG, as well.
Did you ensure the PPG contains diverse perspectives from the implementers?When composing a PPG, a common pitfall is to recruit volunteers from the pool of potential implementers as PPG members. However, such volunteers tend to be positively biased towards the initiative from the outset, and therefore may not be in the best position to provide a critical perspective. For example, they may overestimate how easily peers will implement the intervention, or they may not be familiar with all practical obstacles people encounter. Therefore, make sure that your PPG also contains prospective implementers who are not unequivocally positive.
Did you define at-risk population?In your description of the at-risk population (the population that will benefit from this intervention), it is important to clearly describe the population in terms of a combination of characteristics, e.g. age, gender, etc.
Did you define target population (will often be the same as the at-risk population)?Similarly, in your description of the target population (the focal population of this intervention, in other words, the population that will be targetted by this intervention), it is also important to clearly describe the population in terms of a combination of characteristics, e.g. age, gender, etc.
Did you define Quality of Life of at-risk population?In your description of the health problem, it is important to clearly define the most relevant variable(s) compromising the Quality of Life for this at-risk population and society.
Did you assess the quality of life of at-risk population?In your description of the Quality of Life of the at-risk population, it is important to describe the level of Quality of Life in the at-risk population for each variable that indicates quality of life.
Did you define (health) problem(s) of at-risk population?In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to clearly describe what the health risk or problem is. Basic questions such as 'what is the problem?', 'who has it?' assist you in defining the health problem.
Did you assess prevalence of problem(s) in at-risk population?In your description of the prevalence, frequency and/or intensity of the at-risk population, it is important to describe the assessment of the prevalence for each (health) problem.
Did you define undesirable behavior(s) of target population?In your description of behavior(s) of the target population(s) it is important to describe the undesirable behavior(s) in light of the health problem(s) of the at-risk population.Undesirable behavior(s) are those behavior(s) you want to change with your intervention.
Did you assess the extent of undesirable behavior in target population?In your description of the undesirable behavior(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to assess the extent in which these behavior(s) are present in target population (e.g., by measuring the prevalence, frequency, intensity).
Did you define environmental factors contributing to problem(s)?In your description of the health problem(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to define environmental factors contributing to these problem(s).
Did you identify environmental agent(s) of environmental factor(s) of problem?In your description of the health problem(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to identify the environmental agent(s) who control the previously described environmental factor(s) of the problem.
Did you assess environmental factors contributing to problem(s)?In your description of the environmental factors of the at-risk population, it is important to describe the assessment of each environment factor contributing to the (health)problem(s) of the target population.
Did you define environmental factors contributing to QOL?In your description of environmental factor(s) contributing to the undesirable behavior(s), it is important to define to what extend these are contributing to the quality of life of the at-risk population.
Did you identify environmental agent(s) of environmental factor(s) of QOL?In your description of the health problem(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to identify the environmental agent(s) who control the previously described environmental factor(s) of Quality of Life of the at-risk population.
Did you assess environmental factors contributing to QOL?In your description of the environmental factors of the at-risk population, it is important to describe the assessment of each environment factor contributing to the Quality of Life in the at-risk population.
Did you define environmental factors contributing to undesirable behavior?In your description of the undesirable behavior(s) contributing to health problem(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to define environmental factors contributing to these undesirable behavior(s).
Did you identify environmental agent(s) of environmental factor(s) of undesirable behavior?In your description of the health problem(s) of the at-risk population, it is important to identify the environmental agent(s) who control the previously described environmental factor(s) of the undesirable behavior(s) of the at-risk population.
Did you assess environmental factors contributing to undesirable behavior(s)?In your description of the environmental factors of the at-risk population, it is important to describe the assessment of each environment factor contributing to undesirable behavior(s) of the target population.
Did you justify (health) problem(s)-Quality of Life link(s)?In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the (health) problem(s) of the at-risk population and the Quality of Life of the at-risk population.
Did you justify behavior(s)-problem(s) link(s)?In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the (health) problem(s) of the at-risk population and the undesirable behavior(s) of the target population.
Did you justify behavior(s)-QOL link(s)?In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the undesirable behavior(s) of the target population and the Quality of Life of the at-risk population.
Did you justify environmental factor(s)-health problem(s) link(s)?In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the environmental factor(s) and the (health) problem(s) of the at-risk population.
Did you justify environmental factor(s)-undesirable behavior(s) link(s)?In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the undesirable behavior(s) of the target population and the environmental factor(s).
Did you justify environmental factor(s)-QOL link(s)?In your description of the (health) problem(s), it is important to jusitify and explain causal links between the environmental factor(s) and the Quality of Life of the at-risk population.
Did you generate a logic model of the problem?A logic model of the problem presents a conceptual map containing all relevant factors related to the (health) problem(s) you identified.
Did you identify the target community capacity?In your description of the target community capacity, it is important to identify a list of the assets contributing to the capacity of the target population.
Did you assess capacity of target community?In your description of the target community capacity, it is important to describe the assessment of the extent of the capacity of the target population.
Did you identify target community environmental assets?In your description of the target community capacity, it is important to identify a list of the assets contributing to environmental assests of the target population.
Did you assess available environmental assets of target community?In your description of the available environmental assets of the target community, it is important to describe the ubiquity of each available environmental asset.
Did you define program goals?In your description of program goals, it is important to define goals in terms of Quality of Life, (health) problem, behavior, relevant environmental factor, and/or determinant of behavior.
Did you justify program goals?In your description of program goals, it is important to justify the goals by describing any explanation of the reasoning underlying each program goal.
Did you define specific program goals?In your description of program goals, it is important to define the program goals as specific as possible.
Did you define measurable program goals?In your description of program goals, it is important to define how the program goals are measurable (this also related to the evaluation).
Did you define temporally specific program goals?Similarly, in your description of program goals it is also important to define temporally specific progam goals. In other words, it needs to be clear in which period/term the goals are to be achieved.
Did you make a decision how to evaluate the program goals?In your description of program goals, it it important to describe the methodology, operationalisations, from whom data will be gathered, and execution of evaluating the goals. Moreover, it is important to justify if this aspect of the intervention will not be evaluated.
Did you define the desirable outcome?A desirable outcome of an intervention can be for example a behavioral, health and/or QOL outcome for the at-risk population, achieving realising an environmental outcome.
Did you justify the desirable outcome?In your description of the desirable outcome, it is important to justify selecting this desirable outcome. Such a justification can be, for example, a high prevalence of a health problem, or indications of suffering as a consequence of a health problem or environmental condition. For desirable outcomes in supporting interventions, i.e. interventions that emerge from other intervention development processes, for example to change environmental conditions, the justification can simply be the statement of their inclusion in the 'parent intervention' (i.e. the intervention from which this desirable outcome emerged).
Did you define the target entity?The target entity are the people at risk or environmental agents whom you want to target with the program. In your description of the target entity (e.g. the target population or the environmental agent), it is important to clearly describe the entity in terms of a combination of characteristics, e.g. age, gender, etc.
Did you select the desirable outcome?This item is only relevant when completing the checklist for a supporting intervention. In those cases, it is often necessary to select one or more desirable outcomes (e.g. behaviors of implementing agents, or environmental conditions) because not all identified potential desirable outcomes can be targeted due to lack of funds. Here, indicate whether you justified the selection of this environmental/implementation outcome or agent on the basis of relevance and changeability (note: can be empty for the main intervention outcome, which is implicitly selected).
Did you decide whether to evaluate the desirable outcome?To determine the impact of the program it is important to decide whether to evaluate the desirable outcome, and then specify either the operationalisations, or a justification as to why this desirable outcome will not be evaluated.
Did you define the desirable behavior(s) of the target entity?A desirable behavior is the behavior you want to change of the stated target entity. In your description of desirable behavior(s), it is important to clearly describe the behavior(s) of the target entity that are desirable in light of the desirable outcome (e.g. health and/or QOL of the at-risk population, realising an environmental outcome, etc). This is the behavior that the intervention will aim to promote.
Did you justify the desirable behavior?Just like it is important to justify the desirable outcome, it is important to justify how the desirable behavior contributes to the ultimate desirable outcome. Such a justification can be, for example, an explanation of how this behavior, if performed by the target entity, will contribute to, for example, the at-risk population's health or QOL, or to realising the relevant environmental outcome.
Did you decide on whether to evaluate the desirable behavior(s)?To determine the impact of the program it is important to decide whether to evaluate the desirable behavior(s), and document either the operationalisation, or a justification as to why this desirable behavior will not be evaluated.
Did you define sub-behavior(s) for each behavioral outcome?When we speak of 'behaviors', we often use an umbrella term that, on closer inspection, appears to comprise multiple sub-behaviors (called performance objectives). It is important to think about these performance objectives for every specified desirable behavior and list and define them. For example, 'condom use' often consists of obtaining condoms (e.g. buying them), carrying them, discussing their use in a timely fashion, and using them correctly. These performance objectives have different determinants, and so it is important to distinguish them.
Did you justify the link between the sub-behavior(s) and the desirable behavioral outcome(s)?In your description of the sub-behavior(s) (performance objectives), it is important to provide an explanation of the causal relationship between the performance objective(s) and the desirable behavior(s). How do you know that performing this sub-behavior is a requirement for the desirable behavior? Sometimes common sense suffices; sometimes these links emerge through dedicated studies, for example using observation methods.
Did you select which sub-behaviors (performance objectives) you will target in your intervention?Limited resources (e.g. time, funds) often mean that choices have to be made regarding intervention targets. Make sure these choices are clearly documented and supported by a justification. Often, anticipated relevance (to the desirable behavior) and changeability are important factors in such decisions.
Did you formulate the sub-behavior(s) (performance objectives) according to the guidelines?Performance objectives (i.e. sub-behaviors) can be tricky to define properly. Guidelines exist to aid in their exact formulation. Specifically, performance objective(s) must be formulated in terms of an observable activity that individuals need to complete to perform the overarching desired behavior(s).
Did you decide whether to evaluate the performance objectives?Like with other intervention objectives, it is important to make an explicit and justified decision as to whether to evaluate each performance objective. Again, specify either the operationalisations that will be used, or a justification as to why a performance objective will not be evaluated.
Did you define the environmental outcomes that influence each performance objective (i.e. each sub-behavior)?Behaviors of target entities (e.g. target population members, implementers, environmental agents) are influenced both by their psychology (captures in the behavior's determinants) and their environment. Therefore, for each performance objective, list and describe the environmental conditions that optimize the likelihood that the behavior is performed (i.e. the enviromental outcomes). Each identified environmental outcome is itself a desirable outcome of your intervention project, so for each environmental outcome, you will decide whether to select it for intervention or not, and if you do select it, you will identify the relevant environmental agent(s), their desired behavioral outcome(s), performance objective(s), determinants, etc.
Did you define determinants for each of the sub-behavior(s) (performance objectives)?For each determinant you identified as determining the associated performance objective, provide a definition. This definition can often be lifted from the relevant psychological theory.
Did you justify the link between the determinant(s) and the sub-behavior(s) (performance objectives)?Describe the theoretical or empirical evidence that each determinant does indeed contribute to behavior. Ideally, this evidence pertains to the relevant performance objective(s) and the selected target population.
Did you select the determinant(s)?Limited resources (e.g. time, funds) often mean that choices have to be made regarding intervention targets. Make sure these choices are clearly documented and supported by a justification. Often, determinant importance and changeability of the determinant are important factors in such decisions. Importance and changeability are, unfortunately, often negatively correlated. For example, knowledge is relatively easy to change but usually not important for behavior, whereas skills and self-efficacy are often very important for behavior but much harder (more expensive) to change.
Did you formulate the determinant(s) according to the definition?Because determinants are explicitly defined as modifiable psychological variables, that means it is important to consider how exactly you define them. Often, definitions can be lifted from psychological theory, which will usually ensure that they indeed define determinants are modifiable psychological variables. In any case, make sure the determinant can be feasible changed with an intervention of the kind you have resources for. For example, depression, while psychological and in theory modifiable, is often not feasibly considered as modifiable in the context of a behavior change intervention: successfully alleviating depression requires an investment that is usually incommensurate to the ultimate desirable outcome (e.g. decreasing binge drinking).
Did you decide on evaluating the determinants?Like with other intervention objectives, it is important to make an explicit and justified decision as to whether to evaluate each determinant. Again, specify either the operationalisations that will be used, or a justification as to why a determinant will not be evaluated.
Did you define sub-determinants for each determinant of the sub-behavior(s) (performance objectives)?Because sub-determinants are aspects of determinants, defining them is often easier, but still very important. Often, theories will already provide guidelines. For example, the Reasoned Action Approach holds that attitude consists of attitudinal beliefs, each of which is the product of, on the one hand, people's perceived likelihood of a given consequence of performing the behavior, and on the other hand, their evaluation of that consequence in terms of desirability.
Did you justify the link between the sub-determinant(s) and the determinant(s)?In your description of the sub-determinant(s), it is important to provide any explanation of the causal relationship between the sub-determinant(s) and the determinant(s)
Did you formulate the sub-determinant(s) as change objective(s)?In your description of sub-determinant(s), it is important to formulate the sub-determinant(s) in terms of change objectives. For example, attitudinal beliefs should be formulated as if they are already being held by the target entity. So, the sub-determinant, in this case a belief, that captures the knowledge that going to bed on time enhanced one's ability to concentrate better the next day would be formulated as "A parent between 25 and 50 years of age explains how going to bed on time enhances their ability to concentrate better the next day". In other words, Change Objectives are SMARTly formulated statements, which, if held, mean that in the target entity's psychology, the associated sub-determinant has the desired value (e.g. an at-risk individual holds the desired belief).
Did you formulate the change objective(s) according to the definition?Each change objective concerns a specification of an aspect of a determinant of a performance objective. In your description of change objective(s), it is important to formulate the objective(s) as an action that, when exhibited by the target population, evidences the desired value of the sub-determinant (for example, using the action verbs from the IM book). In other words, change objectives are SMART goals that, when achieved, guarantee a relevant change in the corresponding determinant. It is important to check each change objective to make sure that it still contains the relevant sub-determinant (and nothing else) and that it is SMARTly formulated.
Did you decide on evaluating the change objectives?Like with other intervention objectives, it is important to make an explicit and justified decision as to whether to evaluate each change objective. Again, specify either the operationalisations that will be used, or a justification as to why a change objective will not be evaluated.
Did you describe change objectives in at least one application, and targeted by at least one method?To observe if any of the previously defined change objectives have been missed, it is important to describe how the change objectives are linked to the selected methods and application(s).
Did you describe all change objectives in at least one program message?To observe if any of the previously defined change objectives have been missed, it is important to describe how the change objectives are covered in the program messages. Again, tools such as the ABCD or the underlying matrix can help.
Did you identify and select methods of behavior change at the individual level for each determinant?To influence changes in determinants of behaviors of the target group it is important to identify and select evidence-based change methods. For each determinant there are methods available which you can select.
Did you justify the implied potential for each method of behavior change targeting the target population?For the methods that are selected, it is important to document the reasons your have to believe that that method will be able to change the determinant(s) it targets. Therefore, document any theoretical, empirical, or other evidence you have to justify this selection.
Did you explain how the method of behavior change at the individual level is translated to one or more practical application(s)?In additition to the selection of methods, it is important to decide which application(s) best fit the situation and context at the individual level. Applications are literally how behavior change methods are applied: they represent the translation of theoretical behavior change methods into practice.
Did you describe how each application on individual level takes into account population, cultural, and contextual factors?The most promising practical application(s) of methods differ based on population, cultural, and contextual factors. It is important to describe how each application takes these factors into account.
Did you describe a list of parameters for each method at the individual level, or an explanation as to why a method has not parameters?Translating methods into practical applications succesfully depends on the correct use of parameters (i.e., under which conditions the methods are most effective). It is important to pay close attention to the parameters of use before applying, and this requires consulting evidence and theory, such as is discussed in the Intervention Mapping book and available in bibliographic databases.
Did you describe how each parameter of each method is satisfied in the corresponding application?Once the list of parameters has been identified, describe how each is satisfied in the translation of the relevant methods to the applications.
Did you identify and select methods of behavior change at the environmental level for each determinant?To influence changes in determinants of behaviors of the environmental agent it is important to identify and select evidence-based change methods. For each determinant there are methods available which you can select.
Did you justify the implied potential for each method of behavioral change targetting an environmental agent?For the methods that are selected, it is important to document the reasons your have to believe that that method will be able to change the determinant(s) it targets. Therefore, document any theoretical, empirical, or other evidence you have to justify this selection.
Did you explain how the method of behavior change at the environmental level is translated to one or more practical application(s)?In additition to the selection of methods, it is important to decide which application(s) best fit the situation and context at the environmental level. Applications are literally how behavior change methods are applied: they represent the translation of theoretical behavior change methods into practice.
Did you describe how each application on environmental level takes into account population, cultural, and contextual factors?The most promising practical application(s) of methods differ based on population, cultural, and contextual factors. It is important to describe how each application takes these factors into account.
Did you describe a list of parameters for each method at the environmental level, or an explanation as to why a method has not parameters?Translating methods into practical applications succesfully depends on the correct use of parameters (i.e., under which conditions the methods are most effective). It is important to pay close attention to the parameters of use before applying, and this requires consulting evidence and theory, such as is discussed in the Intervention Mapping book and available in bibliographic databases.
Did you describe how each parameter of each method is satisfied in the corresponding application?Once the list of parameters has been identified, describe how each is satisfied in the translation of the relevant methods to the applications.
Did you generate the matrix of change objectives for this target entity?A matrix of change objectives presents an overview stating what should change at the determinant level for the target entity. Change objectives can be formulated when you cross performance objectives with their determinants. Therefore, you have to build matrices for every health promoting behavior, every target group, and every environmental agent.
Did you generate a logic model of change?In addition to the logic model in step 1, the logic model of change represents pathways of program effects. The model of change defines what the intervention needs to change to reduce risk (or to promote, protect, and improve health) for the problem. Therefore, the model includes the desired behavioral outcome(s) and all relevant factors (agents, performance objectives, determinants, change objectices, environmental conditions)
Did you generate program theme(s)?A program theme is a general organizing construct or idea for your program.Themes, together with several recurring visual and linguistic subthemes or ideas, can support the delivery of the health message to a broader audience (not only the primary target audience, but also a secondary audience).Themes can be based on the health topic, the behavioral or community change objectives, or be unrelated to the program content.
Did you discuss the theme(s) with the PPG?Because generating thoughts on theme(s) is a participatory process, it is imperative to discuss theme(s) and the ways to use them with your PPG.
Did you define the program scope?Program components consist of identifiable scope and sequences. During this process you define who, what, when, where, how much, and how often. To decide what is included in the program and what not it is advisable to decide upon the scope of your program. Scope refers to the breadth and amount of a program, and defines what is, and what is not in the program.
Did you discuss the scope with the PPG?Because generating thoughts on the scope of your program is a participatory process, it is imperative to discuss the scope and the ways to use them with your PPG.
Did you define the program sequence?In addition to the program scope, it is also important to define the order of the components, as well as the duration of each component.
Did you discuss the sequence with PPG?Because generating thoughts on the sequence of your program is a participatory process, it is imperative to discuss the sequence and the ways to use them with your PPG.
Did you select delivery channel(s) and vehicle(s)?Besides themes it is also important to decide and select on how you want to deliver the message(s). A communication channel can be interpersonal or mediated. On the other hand, a vehicle is how the message is packed and delivered. The selection is based on the most preferred and accepted media use of your intended target population.
Did you discuss the delivery channel(s) and vehicle(s) with PPG?Because generating thoughts on delivery channel(s) and vehicle(s) is a participatory process, it is imperative to discuss the sequence and the ways to use them with your PPG.
Did you define program components?Because intervention programs often have units or modules (components), at some point these need to be explicity defined. Components can be combinations of behavior change methods, practical applications, and delivery mechanisms aimed at various objectives.
Did you verify how each parameter of use for each method is still satisfied in the corresponding application?Because the parameters of use are crucial for intervention effectiveness, it is important to make sure you verify them in the final applications.
Did you select which theoretical conditions to pretest in each component?It is important to pretest the theoretical conditions so as to be able to make corrections if necessary. However, resources usually lack to pretest everything, and therefore, it is important to select which conditions to pretest.
Have you selected the operationalisation(s) that will be used for the evaluation, survey, or experiment?An operationalisation is what is used to measure (or manipulate) the variable of interest. This is often a questionnaire, but other measures such as blood values can also be used.
Have you determined from whom you will gather data?In evaluations, the data are often provided by the target populations, for example, when measuring determinants. However, sometimes it can be useful if another group provides the data; for example, parents or teachers can provide data about their children, or physicians about their patients.
Have you selected the methodology to use for this evaluation, survey, or experiment?Most research questions can be answered with a number of different methodologies. Did you identify existing materials were identified, and if necessary, adapt them to your local situation?Often, existing interventions can be re-used entirely, or valuable components can be re-used. Given the investment required to develop a theory- and evidence-based intervention, it is wise to identify existing materials and re-use what you can, rather than always starting from scratch and engaging in costly re-inventions of the wheel.
Did you safeguard the fidelity of the change objectives, parameters, and messages during supervision of the program production?Executive professionals can sometimes take certain creative liberties that jeopardize intervention effectiveness, for example where they concern changes that lead to omission of change objectives or messages, or to violation of the parameters of use. It is therefore important to stay vigilant and direct the executive professionals where necessary. The design documents can support this process.
Did you review the components, and did you reconsider and if necessary, refine them?During the tasks in step 4, it is possible that alignment between methods, applications, parameters, and change objectives shifts or is lost in translation. It is therefore important to describe if and how you reviewed, reconsidered, and refined the program structure and organization. Tools such as the ABCD and the underlying matrix can support this task.
Did you define the program message(s) on the basis of the change objectives?Messages are focused attempts to effectively promote and accomplish a change objective. They are the verbal or visual elements of the intervention that target the specific change objectes.
Did you develop the design documents?A design document provides information on (i) the length of the product, a brief description of the audience, the way the user will interact with the product, the purpose of the piece, the central messages, and the expected budget; (ii) and or an overview of change objectives as they pertain to each material or activity. The design documents are used in briefings for the executive professionals such as advertising agencies, copywriters, designers, and website developers. They are also used to properly supervise them during the process to ensure that all change objectives remain addressed and all parameters for use remain respected.
Did you identify relevant adopters?Adopters are the entities (persons, organisations, or organisational bodies) that have to make one or more decisions to enable program implementation. It is important to identify these stakeholders as early as possible so as to enable discussing the program with them and make adjustments if necessary.
Did you discuss program components with adopters?In addition to identifying the adopters, it is important to discuss the program with them.
Did you identify relevant implementers?Implementers are the entities (persons, organisations, or organisational bodies) that have to perform one or more behaviors to enable program implementation. Examples are teachers; nurses; physicians; or outreach workers. It is important to identify these stakeholders as early as possible so as to enable discussing the program with them and make adjustments if necessary.
Did you discuss program components with implementers?In addition to identifying the implementers, it is important to discuss the program with them.
Did you identify relevant maintainers?Maintainers are the entities (persons, organisations, or organisational bodies) that have to make one or more decisions or perform one or more behaviors to enable program maintenance. Examples are school boards; teachers; hospital directors; nurses; physicians; or outreach workers. It is important to identify these stakeholders as early as possible so as to enable discussing the program with them and make adjustments if necessary.
Did you discuss program components with maintainers?In addition to identifying the maintainers, it is important to discuss the program with them.
Did you produce documentation to enable accurate implementation of the intervention?During intervention development, you take many decisions and achieve implicit shared understanding about many things with all involved. However, this shared understanding will fade over time; and others may want to implement your intervention in different settings. Therefore, it is important to produce accurate and exhaustive documentation, documenting your decisions and justifications as well as the results of those decisions.
Did you define the desirable outcome?A desirable outcome of an intervention can be for example a behavioral, health and/or QOL outcome for the at-risk population, achieving realising an environmental outcome.
Did you justify the desirable outcome?In your description of the desirable outcome, it is important to justify selecting this desirable outcome. Such a justification can be, for example, a high prevalence of a health problem, or indications of suffering as a consequence of a health problem or environmental condition. For desirable outcomes in supporting interventions, i.e. interventions that emerge from other intervention development processes, for example to change environmental conditions, the justification can simply be the statement of their inclusion in the 'parent intervention' (i.e. the intervention from which this desirable outcome emerged).
Did you define the target entity?The target entity are the people at risk or environmental agents whom you want to target with the program. In your description of the target entity (e.g. the target population or the environmental agent), it is important to clearly describe the entity in terms of a combination of characteristics, e.g. age, gender, etc.
Did you select the desirable outcome?This item is only relevant when completing the checklist for a supporting intervention. In those cases, it is often necessary to select one or more desirable outcomes (e.g. behaviors of implementing agents, or environmental conditions) because not all identified potential desirable outcomes can be targeted due to lack of funds. Here, indicate whether you justified the selection of this environmental/implementation outcome or agent on the basis of relevance and changeability (note: can be empty for the main intervention outcome, which is implicitly selected).
Did you decide whether to evaluate the desirable outcome?To determine the impact of the program it is important to decide whether to evaluate the desirable outcome, and then specify either the operationalisations, or a justification as to why this desirable outcome will not be evaluated.
Did you define the desirable behavior(s) of the target entity?A desirable behavior is the behavior you want to change of the stated target entity. In your description of desirable behavior(s), it is important to clearly describe the behavior(s) of the target entity that are desirable in light of the desirable outcome (e.g. health and/or QOL of the at-risk population, realising an environmental outcome, etc). This is the behavior that the intervention will aim to promote.
Did you justify the desirable behavior?Just like it is important to justify the desirable outcome, it is important to justify how the desirable behavior contributes to the ultimate desirable outcome. Such a justification can be, for example, an explanation of how this behavior, if performed by the target entity, will contribute to, for example, the at-risk population's health or QOL, or to realising the relevant environmental outcome.
Did you decide on whether to evaluate the desirable behavior(s)?To determine the impact of the program it is important to decide whether to evaluate the desirable behavior(s), and document either the operationalisation, or a justification as to why this desirable behavior will not be evaluated.
Did you define sub-behavior(s) for each behavioral outcome?When we speak of 'behaviors', we often use an umbrella term that, on closer inspection, appears to comprise multiple sub-behaviors (called performance objectives). It is important to think about these performance objectives for every specified desirable behavior and list and define them. For example, 'condom use' often consists of obtaining condoms (e.g. buying them), carrying them, discussing their use in a timely fashion, and using them correctly. These performance objectives have different determinants, and so it is important to distinguish them.
Did you justify the link between the sub-behavior(s) and the desirable behavioral outcome(s)?In your description of the sub-behavior(s) (performance objectives), it is important to provide an explanation of the causal relationship between the performance objective(s) and the desirable behavior(s). How do you know that performing this sub-behavior is a requirement for the desirable behavior? Sometimes common sense suffices; sometimes these links emerge through dedicated studies, for example using observation methods.
Did you select which sub-behaviors (performance objectives) you will target in your intervention?Limited resources (e.g. time, funds) often mean that choices have to be made regarding intervention targets. Make sure these choices are clearly documented and supported by a justification. Often, anticipated relevance (to the desirable behavior) and changeability are important factors in such decisions.
Did you formulate the sub-behavior(s) (performance objectives) according to the guidelines?Performance objectives (i.e. sub-behaviors) can be tricky to define properly. Guidelines exist to aid in their exact formulation. Specifically, performance objective(s) must be formulated in terms of an observable activity that individuals need to complete to perform the overarching desired behavior(s).
Did you decide whether to evaluate the performance objectives?Like with other intervention objectives, it is important to make an explicit and justified decision as to whether to evaluate each performance objective. Again, specify either the operationalisations that will be used, or a justification as to why a performance objective will not be evaluated.
Did you define the environmental outcomes that influence each performance objective (i.e. each sub-behavior)?Behaviors of target entities (e.g. target population members, implementers, environmental agents) are influenced both by their psychology (captures in the behavior's determinants) and their environment. Therefore, for each performance objective, list and describe the environmental conditions that optimize the likelihood that the behavior is performed (i.e. the enviromental outcomes). Each identified environmental outcome is itself a desirable outcome of your intervention project, so for each environmental outcome, you will decide whether to select it for intervention or not, and if you do select it, you will identify the relevant environmental agent(s), their desired behavioral outcome(s), performance objective(s), determinants, etc.
Did you define determinants for each of the sub-behavior(s) (performance objectives)?For each determinant you identified as determining the associated performance objective, provide a definition. This definition can often be lifted from the relevant psychological theory.
Did you justify the link between the determinant(s) and the sub-behavior(s) (performance objectives)?Describe the theoretical or empirical evidence that each determinant does indeed contribute to behavior. Ideally, this evidence pertains to the relevant performance objective(s) and the selected target population.
Did you select the determinant(s)?Limited resources (e.g. time, funds) often mean that choices have to be made regarding intervention targets. Make sure these choices are clearly documented and supported by a justification. Often, determinant importance and changeability of the determinant are important factors in such decisions. Importance and changeability are, unfortunately, often negatively correlated. For example, knowledge is relatively easy to change but usually not important for behavior, whereas skills and self-efficacy are often very important for behavior but much harder (more expensive) to change.
Did you formulate the determinant(s) according to the definition?Because determinants are explicitly defined as modifiable psychological variables, that means it is important to consider how exactly you define them. Often, definitions can be lifted from psychological theory, which will usually ensure that they indeed define determinants are modifiable psychological variables. In any case, make sure the determinant can be feasible changed with an intervention of the kind you have resources for. For example, depression, while psychological and in theory modifiable, is often not feasibly considered as modifiable in the context of a behavior change intervention: successfully alleviating depression requires an investment that is usually incommensurate to the ultimate desirable outcome (e.g. decreasing binge drinking).
Did you decide on evaluating the determinants?Like with other intervention objectives, it is important to make an explicit and justified decision as to whether to evaluate each determinant. Again, specify either the operationalisations that will be used, or a justification as to why a determinant will not be evaluated.
Did you define sub-determinants for each determinant of the sub-behavior(s) (performance objectives)?Because sub-determinants are aspects of determinants, defining them is often easier, but still very important. Often, theories will already provide guidelines. For example, the Reasoned Action Approach holds that attitude consists of attitudinal beliefs, each of which is the product of, on the one hand, people's perceived likelihood of a given consequence of performing the behavior, and on the other hand, their evaluation of that consequence in terms of desirability.
Did you justify the link between the sub-determinant(s) and the determinant(s)?In your description of the sub-determinant(s), it is important to provide any explanation of the causal relationship between the sub-determinant(s) and the determinant(s)
Did you formulate the sub-determinant(s) as change objective(s)?In your description of sub-determinant(s), it is important to formulate the sub-determinant(s) in terms of change objectives. For example, attitudinal beliefs should be formulated as if they are already being held by the target entity. So, the sub-determinant, in this case a belief, that captures the knowledge that going to bed on time enhanced one's ability to concentrate better the next day would be formulated as "A parent between 25 and 50 years of age explains how going to bed on time enhances their ability to concentrate better the next day". In other words, Change Objectives are SMARTly formulated statements, which, if held, mean that in the target entity's psychology, the associated sub-determinant has the desired value (e.g. an at-risk individual holds the desired belief).
Did you formulate the change objective(s) according to the definition?Each change objective concerns a specification of an aspect of a determinant of a performance objective. In your description of change objective(s), it is important to formulate the objective(s) as an action that, when exhibited by the target population, evidences the desired value of the sub-determinant (for example, using the action verbs from the IM book). In other words, change objectives are SMART goals that, when achieved, guarantee a relevant change in the corresponding determinant. It is important to check each change objective to make sure that it still contains the relevant sub-determinant (and nothing else) and that it is SMARTly formulated.
Did you decide on evaluating the change objectives?Like with other intervention objectives, it is important to make an explicit and justified decision as to whether to evaluate each change objective. Again, specify either the operationalisations that will be used, or a justification as to why a change objective will not be evaluated.
Did you describe change objectives in at least one application, and targeted by at least one method?To observe if any of the previously defined change objectives have been missed, it is important to describe how the change objectives are linked to the selected methods and application(s).
Did you describe all change objectives in at least one program message?To observe if any of the previously defined change objectives have been missed, it is important to describe how the change objectives are covered in the program messages. Again, tools such as the ABCD or the underlying matrix can help.
Did you identify and select methods of behavior change at the individual level for each determinant?To influence changes in determinants of behaviors of the target group it is important to identify and select evidence-based change methods. For each determinant there are methods available which you can select.
Did you justify the implied potential for each method of behavior change targeting the target population?For the methods that are selected, it is important to document the reasons your have to believe that that method will be able to change the determinant(s) it targets. Therefore, document any theoretical, empirical, or other evidence you have to justify this selection.
Did you explain how the method of behavior change at the individual level is translated to one or more practical application(s)?In additition to the selection of methods, it is important to decide which application(s) best fit the situation and context at the individual level. Applications are literally how behavior change methods are applied: they represent the translation of theoretical behavior change methods into practice.
Did you describe how each application on individual level takes into account population, cultural, and contextual factors?The most promising practical application(s) of methods differ based on population, cultural, and contextual factors. It is important to describe how each application takes these factors into account.
Did you describe a list of parameters for each method at the individual level, or an explanation as to why a method has not parameters?Translating methods into practical applications succesfully depends on the correct use of parameters (i.e., under which conditions the methods are most effective). It is important to pay close attention to the parameters of use before applying, and this requires consulting evidence and theory, such as is discussed in the Intervention Mapping book and available in bibliographic databases.
Did you describe how each parameter of each method is satisfied in the corresponding application?Once the list of parameters has been identified, describe how each is satisfied in the translation of the relevant methods to the applications.

For each determinant there are methods available which you can select.
Did you justify the implied potential for each method of behavioral change targetting an environmental agent?For the methods that are selected, it is important to document the reasons your have to believe that that method will be able to change the determinant(s) it targets. Glossary (click here to toggle definitions)
Adopter An entity that has the power to decide that a program will be used in a given context.
Agent Individuals or groups in positions to exercise control over (aspects of) the environment.
Assess Determine the value of one or more variables
At-risk population The population that will benefit from this intervention (e.g. 'infants').
Component A component is a product of the actual intervention development process, incorporating one or more applications. The components are discrete intervention units or modules that together form the intervention.
Define Specify what (which variables) was (or were) used/chosen/selected
Delivery channels and vehicles The vehicle is the medium carrying the intervention, for example text, audio, video, and/or interpersonal communication. Old archaic definition of "channel" was that it was either "interpersonal" (of the intervention is delivered to the target population members by an individual) or "mediated" (all other methods to deliver the intervention).
Design document A design document is a document that describes which messages are included in which program components. It conveys the program planning team's intentions to those who write or select content from available resources and design graphics and other communications. They function as guidance for the executive producers of the program, e.g. advertising agencies, copywriters, programmers, art directors, etc.
Desirable Outcome Whichever ultimate outcome is the assumed causal consequence of the target entity engaging in the target behavior (e.g. health and/or QOL of the at-risk population, realising an environmental outcome, realising a condition for successful implementation, etc)
Determinant A modifiable psychological variable
Element NA
Entity Any individual or organisation that can be targeted by an intervention. Therefore, 'entity' can refer to both target population individuals, environmental agents, and implementers. The target entity can be, for example, a school board, a nurse, or an obese adolescent.
Environmental factors Aspects of the environment which influences the behavior
Health In this checklist, we define health as a state where somebody is not impaired by (physical or mental) disease. Note that this definition is (deliberately) much more narrow than the 1948 WHO definition, where health is defined as 'a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity'. The aspects included in this WHO definition but omitted from our current definition of health are included in the concept of Quality of Life.
Implementer An entity that has the responsibility to use a program (at the operational level) in a given context.
Logic Model A visual representation of the relevant variables and the hypothesized causal relationships between those variables
Main intervention An intervention targeting a target population individual; this is the intervention that an intervention developer usually starts with. During this process, an intervention developer will often need to develop supporting interventions, which target environmental agents or implementation agents.
Maintainers An entity that has the power to decide that a program will be institutionalised (i.e. used structurally and embedded in an infrastructure's routines).
Participatory Planning Group (PPG) A participatory planning group allows participation by community members, potential program implementers, and program beneficiaries in the planning proces.
Performance objective A sub-behavior of the target behavior. Performance objectives are distinguished on the basis of their (dis)similarity in terms of the (sub-)determinants and environmental factors that predict them: only if two sub-behaviors are predicted by different determinants and/or environmental factors, they are considered separate performance objectives.
Production team The external professionals who do the executive production of a program, e.g. advertising agencies, copywriters, programmers, art directors, etc.
Program goal Program goals are objectives which provide a focus while developing the program. These goals need to be realistic and measureable.
Quality of Life (QOL) The general state of well-being (health, comfort, and happiness) experienced by an individual or group.
Scope A program scope defines breadth of a program. The scope is a list of domains that are included (targeted) by the program as well as a list of domains that are explicitly not included (targeted) by the program. Note that one of these lists can be redundant.
Sequence For programs that consist of more than one component, the sequence defines in which order these must be presented.
Stakeholder Every person, organisation or role whose involvement in the intervention development process can reasonably be expected to contribute to intervention effectiveness.
Sub-determinant An aspect of a determinant that is sufficiently specific. It is important to note that these determinants have a given level of psychological aggregation. For example, in the RAA, the determinant intention is predicted by the three lower-level variables attitude, perceived norm and perceived behavioural control. Attitude, in turn, can be considered to consist of experiential and instrumental attitude; and each of these consists of behavioural beliefs that differ for each target behaviour and each target group. In other words, determinants of behaviour can be organised on the aggregation level hierarchy in terms of specificity versus generality, and various sub-determinants can often be distinguished for any determinant. Sub-determinants formulated at a high level of specificity (e.g., beliefs in the case of RAA) are crucial when studying determinants, because those very specific aspects are what is used in operationalisations and intervention messages.
Supporting intervention An intervention targeting an environmental agent or an implementation agent. These interventions are usually developed to support the main intervention.
Target population The focal population of this intervention (the populations that will be targeted by the intervention), e.g. 'parents'.
Theme A program theme is a general organising construct or idea for a program that manifests as recurring visual and linguistic patterns. It often includes a program's name and potentially a slogan, the "brand" or "corporate identity" (fonts, colors, logo's), and should be tailored to (sub-)cultural characteristics of the target population.
Variable A measurable characteristic of for example a person, situation, object
